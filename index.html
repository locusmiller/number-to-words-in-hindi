Hindi has one of the most fascinating number systems in the world. Unlike English — where you can predict "thirty-one," "forty-two," "fifty-three" once you learn the pattern — Hindi gives every single number from 1 to 99 its own completely unique name. There's no shortcut. Ek, do, teen give way to gyarah, barah, terah, and then it keeps going from there.

Number to words in Hindi follows a Sanskrit-derived naming system where each number from 1 through 99 has a distinct word, and large numbers use the Lakh and Crore system instead of the Western million and billion format. According to the Census of India 2011, Hindi is spoken by over 600 million people — making it the third most spoken language in the world. That's a lot of people who need to know these number names for everyday tasks like cheque writing, school exams, and government documents.

This guide gives you the complete reference — Devanagari script, Roman transliteration, pronunciation notes, and large number formats up to one crore. Let's get into it.

How Does the Hindi Number System Work?

The Hindi number system is a base-10 system rooted in Sanskrit, where numbers 1 through 99 each carry a unique name with no predictable suffix pattern. This is the biggest difference between Hindi and English counting. In English, once you learn "twenty," you just add "one, two, three" to get twenty-one, twenty-two, and so on. Hindi doesn't work that way — each compound number is its own word entirely.

The Devanagari script is used to write Hindi numbers in their native form. For example, the number 5 is written as पाँच in Devanagari. Most educational materials and government documents in India use both the Devanagari form and the Roman transliteration (Paanch) side by side for accessibility.

Here's how the place value system works in Hindi:

  • Ek (एक) — Ones place (1)
  • Das (दस) — Tens place (10)
  • Sau (सौ) — Hundreds place (100)
  • Hazar (हज़ार) — Thousands place (1,000)
  • Lakh (लाख) — Hundred thousands (1,00,000)
  • Crore (करोड़) — Ten millions (1,00,00,000)

Notice that Hindi skips "million" entirely. This isn't an oversight — it's a deliberate part of the South Asian numbering system that India officially uses. The Government of India uses the Lakh and Crore system in all official documents, budgets, and currency denominations. The Reserve Bank of India prints currency notes and publishes monetary policy reports using Lakh and Crore exclusively.

One more thing worth knowing — Hindi numbers are written left to right in Devanagari script, just like English. The script itself reads left to right, so there's no confusion about reading direction when working with number names.

Numbers 1 to 10 in Hindi Words

The numbers 1 to 10 in Hindi are the foundation of the entire system — learn these ten words and you have the building blocks for every other number. These are the most commonly tested in school exams and the most frequently used in daily speech.

Most of these words come directly from Sanskrit — the classical language that forms the root of Hindi, Urdu, Bengali, Gujarati, and several other South Asian languages. That's why you'll notice similarities between Hindi number words and those in neighboring languages.

One tricky one for beginners is पाँच (Paanch) — that little symbol above the "pa" character (ँ) is called an anusvara and adds a nasal sound. It's what makes Paanch sound slightly different from plain "Panch." In everyday spoken Hindi, both forms are used interchangeably, but the anusvara form is the grammatically correct one.

📌 Note: In school textbooks across Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Madhya Pradesh, and Rajasthan, these 1-to-10 number names are taught as "Hindi Ginti" — which literally means Hindi counting. They appear in Class 1 and Class 2 syllabi across the CBSE and State Board curricula.

Large Numbers in Hindi — Sau, Hazar, Lakh, Crore

Large numbers in Hindi use four key words — Sau (hundred), Hazar (thousand), Lakh (hundred thousand), and Crore (ten million) — instead of the Western million and billion system. This is the official numbering system used by the Government of India, the Reserve Bank of India, and all Indian financial institutions.

The Lakh system trips up a lot of people who are used to the Western format. Here's the key conversion to remember — 1 Lakh = 100,000 and 1 Crore = 10,000,000. So when the Indian government announces a budget of ₹5 Lakh Crore, that's actually ₹50 trillion in Western terms. The numbers sound very different depending on which system you use.

For cheque writing in India specifically, the number to words in Hindi format matters a lot. A cheque for ₹2,50,000 should be written as "Do Lakh Pachaas Hazar Rupees Only" — not "Two Hundred Fifty Thousand Rupees Only." Indian banks process Lakh-based descriptions more smoothly since all their internal systems use the South Asian number format.

Beyond banking, you'll also see Lakh and Crore used in:

  • Indian news media — Population figures, election results, and economic data all use Lakh and Crore
  • CBSE and State Board exams — Math problems from Class 4 onward use the Indian place value system
  • Property transactions — Real estate listings across Indian cities quote prices in Lakh
  • Government tenders — All procurement documents follow the Lakh-Crore system per Ministry of Finance guidelines
💡 Quick Conversion: 1 Million = 10 Lakh. 1 Billion = 100 Crore. 1 Trillion = 1 Lakh Crore. These conversions come up constantly when reading international financial news in an Indian context.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do you say 1000 in Hindi words?

1000 in Hindi is Ek Hazar (एक हज़ार). Hazar is the Hindi word for thousand. So 2000 is Do Hazar, 5000 is Paanch Hazar, and 10,000 is Das Hazar. For amounts above 99,000, the Lakh system takes over — 1,00,000 is Ek Lakh. This naming convention is used in all official Indian government documents, banking records, and CBSE school textbooks.

What is the Hindi word for million?

Hindi does not have a direct word for million. Instead, Hindi uses the Lakh system — one million equals Das Lakh (दस लाख) in Hindi. The Western million and billion system is not part of the standard Hindi number naming convention used in India. The Government of India, the Reserve Bank of India, and all Indian financial institutions officially use Lakh and Crore in place of million and billion.

How are Hindi numbers different from English numbers?

Hindi numbers differ from English numbers in two major ways. First, every number from 1 to 99 in Hindi has a completely unique name with no predictable pattern — unlike English where numbers follow regular rules after twenty. Second, Hindi uses the Lakh and Crore system for large numbers instead of the Western million and billion system. Both differences are rooted in the Sanskrit origins of the Hindi language.
